Creating a Style

To become a skilled photographer, creativity is essential. Developing your creativity means thinking in your own unique way. Use this documentation as a guide to help you begin discovering your style, but remember to branch out and create something that truly reflects your individuality—avoid copying my style or anyone else's!

Constructive criticism is a valuable tool for growth. Any mistakes you make, including those mentioned below, are opportunities to learn and enhance your skills. Embrace the process, and don't be afraid to experiment and evolve as a photographer.

Composition

There are many key components to composition such as rule of thirds, leading lines, and framing.

Rule of Thirds

The rule of thirds is one of the most important principles for creating visually appealing photos. It divides an image into nine equal sections by using two equally spaced horizontal lines and two vertical lines. These lines create a grid that divides the photo into thirds both horizontally and vertically.

To use the rule of thirds effectively, position the main subject or focal points of your photo along these lines or at their intersections (where the lines cross). This helps create a balanced and visually interesting composition that naturally draws the viewer’s eye to key areas of the image.


Generally, you want to avoid placing the subject on the same side as the direction they're facing. For example, if the subject is positioned along the left guideline, they should ideally be facing right. This technique creates a sense of openness and gives the impression that there is space or context in the direction they are looking.

In this example, my subject is positioned on the right line but facing left. This setup creates a visual "barrier," suggesting there is more to explore in front of the subject. Centering the subject is also a strong choice, especially if you want to create balance and draw equal attention to the subject and their surroundings.

Leading Lines

Leading lines are visual elements within a photo that guide the viewer’s eye toward a specific point or subject in the image. These lines often run parallel or converge toward the same point, creating a sense of direction and focus. They can also enhance symmetry, depth, and structure, making the composition more engaging and dynamic. Examples of leading lines include roads, fences, rivers, or architectural features like hallways and staircases.

Framing

Framing is about deciding what to include in your shot and how to position it. Unlike the rule of thirds, which focuses on where the subject lies within the composition, framing emphasizes the relationship between the subject, background, and foreground. It involves using elements within the scene—like windows, doorways, branches, or arches—to create a "frame" around the subject. This technique draws attention to the focal point while adding depth and context to the image.

Bad Photos

Even the best photographers take bad photos—it’s a natural part of the creative process. When a photo doesn’t turn out well, it’s less about how bad it looks and more about identifying what worked and how you can improve next time.

Here’s an example of a bad photo I took, followed by the steps I took to make it better.

To improve this picture, I focused on enhancing its overall composition and tone. The original colors were too harsh, so I opted for a black-and-white edit to create a more balanced and timeless look. I also reframed the shot, positioning the subject in the lower third to add visual interest and better alignment with the rule of thirds.

The lighting in the original photo was quite dark, so I adjusted my shutter speed to 1/60 to let in more light. To maintain stability, I stayed as still as possible and zoomed out to 18mm, capturing more of the scene and adding depth to the photo.

Photography is all about creativity, and it takes time to master. Don’t get discouraged by bad photos—use them as learning opportunities. Take the time to analyze them, understand what went wrong, and think about how you can improve.

Editing

Most photography involves some level of editing in post-production. This step allows you to fine-tune color balance, adjust exposure, and even refine the composition to better tell the story.

In this example, I retained the original composition and captured moment but focused on enhancing the colors and exposure. If you look at the bride’s dress in the unedited photo, you’ll notice how overexposed it appears. By taking advantage of the RAW file format, I was able to recover the details in the dress, ensuring the highlights were well-balanced and the textures were preserved.

Playing with colors and finding your own balance can really help to build your own style.
